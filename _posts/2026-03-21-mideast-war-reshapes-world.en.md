On the last day of February, Trump made what may be the highest-stakes decision of his political career, directly igniting the largest war the Middle East has seen in decades — one that is rapidly reshaping the entire world.

After months of mounting tension, the war officially broke out on February 28. The United States and Israel launched coordinated operations, opening with a ferocious air campaign that killed 86-year-old Khamenei in the very first wave of strikes, and took out scores of Iran's senior officials in subsequent attacks.

## The Scale of the Opening Offensive

Within just six days of the war's outbreak, US forces had conducted thousands of airstrikes across Iran, hitting military installations throughout the country. Iran's navy also suffered heavy losses, with several vessels destroyed or rendered combat-ineffective. US military officials stated that the opening firepower rivals or even surpasses the shock-and-awe intensity of day one of the 2003 Iraq War.

Simultaneously, the Israeli Air Force has been striking Iranian targets at a relentless pace. While claims of hitting 1,000 targets in a single day are clearly exaggerated, the tempo is undeniably dense and the geographic coverage is enormous.

This sustained high-intensity campaign has been possible largely because Israel can now operate over Syrian airspace en route to Iranian targets, with US air refueling tankers providing continuous logistical support.

Human rights organizations have reported thousands of Iranian civilians killed in the US-Israeli strikes. Judging by Israel's operational pattern, its goals go beyond degrading Iran's military — they include driving the entire regime toward systemic collapse through relentless pressure, targeting even the institutions responsible for selecting the next Supreme Leader. The objective is no longer purely military. It is the stability of the regime itself.

The United States, by contrast, has articulated far murkier goals. Trump has called on Iranians to rise up and overthrow their government, yet simultaneously signaled willingness to negotiate. Secretary of State Rubio says the goal is to destroy Iran's missile and naval capabilities. The Secretary of Defense adds the complete elimination of Iran's nuclear program. Even within the US itself, there is no unified answer to how far this war is meant to go.

## Why the War Was Launched — and the Deeper Logic

As for why the war was launched, different factions within the US offer different explanations: Iran was approaching nuclear capability; Israel was going to move anyway and the US just struck first; or intelligence showed Iran was preparing to attack, requiring a preemptive strike. All are plausible. None is entirely convincing.

The deeper logic is a classic geopolitical calculation. The US and Israel concluded that Iran was at its weakest point in decades. Domestically, Iran has been plagued by prolonged drought and water scarcity, a collapsing economy and currency, and large-scale protests. Trump had previously drawn a red line: if the Iranian government harmed protesters, the US would intervene militarily. In practice, even as the situation worsened, the US held back — in part because its military presence in the Middle East was not yet fully assembled.

Once the buildup was complete, the US rapidly concentrated military force in the region at a scale comparable to the eve of the 2003 Iraq War. It then pressed Iran to abandon its nuclear program and missile projects, threatening severe consequences. Eventually that pressure became direct military action.

The greatest danger of this war now is that it is spiraling out of control, threatening to engulf the entire Middle East. It could deliver a devastating blow to the global economy and become a war of attrition harder to end than any previous conflict.

## Iran Strikes Back — and Dubai Burns

Faced with strikes of this magnitude, Iran's response has been fierce. Since the war began, Iran has fired a large number of ballistic missiles and drones, inflicting serious damage on Israeli and American military bases across the Middle East. Many were intercepted, but enough broke through to cause substantial destruction.

On the very first day of the war, Dubai was no longer the safe haven it had been for decades. A luxury hotel on the Palm Jumeirah caught fire. Debris from intercepted missiles rained down on one of Dubai's most iconic landmarks. Areas near the seven-star hotel were engulfed in flame, and fragments fell on residential neighborhoods and near the airport — giving the entire city its first genuine taste of war overhead.

Even more significant was the attack on invisible but critical infrastructure. On the first day, Iranian drones struck an Amazon data center in the Middle East. Days later, another Amazon data center in Bahrain was hit — marking the first time an AI data center had ever been attacked in wartime.

## The End of the Dubai Myth

Dubai's success over the past few decades has rested on one fundamental selling point: it is one of the very few stable, safe places in a turbulent Middle East where business can be done. It connects East and West, serves as the preferred regional headquarters for multinationals, and functions as a haven for global wealth. Gulf royalty, European tax refugees, and Russians and Ukrainians fleeing their own wars have all seen Dubai as a source of security.

It was this myth of stability that drew massive foreign investment in data centers and regional headquarters, giving rise to today's spectacular skyline. But now the war is shaking all of that. When hundreds of missiles and drones fly overhead, interceptors explode in the sky, fighter jets roar past, and landmark buildings catch fire — Dubai's image as a peaceful green zone has been shattered.

In truth, since World War II, very few cities as globally integrated and financially open as Dubai have faced threats of this magnitude in wartime. Once the fighting ends, many foreign companies and residents will almost certainly reassess whether they still want to be there. Insurance costs will rise. Investment will turn cautious. All the UAE government can do right now is project a desperate signal: everything is still under control.

## Air Defense Systems Under Pressure

The deeper problem is the air defense systems themselves. Because Iran fired such massive quantities of missiles and drones in the war's opening phase, Gulf states are facing a very real constraint: they may be running low on interceptors. Air defense systems typically fire two interceptors per incoming missile to ensure a kill. If Iran has fired hundreds of ballistic missiles, defending forces may have consumed close to double that number of interceptors — at roughly five times the consumption rate of the first Gulf War.

Each country's air defense stockpile is a closely guarded secret, because once an adversary knows how many interceptors you have left, they can calculate precisely how many missiles it takes to overwhelm you. The outside world simply doesn't know how much is left in Gulf state arsenals — but the burn rate is clearly unsustainable. Replenishing these missiles takes years and billions of dollars.

If stockpiles are genuinely approaching a critical threshold, these countries may soon face a brutal triage decision: which targets do we protect, and which do we abandon? Should Iran launch another major offensive, they may be forced to prioritize core facilities — oil fields, capital cities, military bases — and leave everything else to chance. This also means these countries may begin pushing hard on Washington to end the war as fast as possible, before their cities and economic arteries can no longer hold.

This also explains why Iran has deliberately spread the war across the entire Middle East. On the surface it looks like retaliation. But the deeper logic is to distribute the cost — to force more and more countries to absorb the pressure together.

Qatar, Bahrain, the UAE, Kuwait, and Saudi Arabia did not directly participate in the strikes on Iran, but they host vast numbers of US military bases, giving Iran a pretext to target them. None of these Gulf states formally authorized the US to use their territory to directly attack Iran — the US has launched most operations from aircraft carriers and Jordan — but that distinction is increasingly academic.

## Energy and Shipping Under Siege

What is Iran's real objective? In one sentence: not simply fighting a war, but fighting an economic war. By attacking cities to spread panic, targeting critical infrastructure, and piling on with an energy shock, Iran aims to put the global economy under maximum stress — and, through that pressure, force Trump to back down.

The energy shock is already materializing. Saudi Arabia's largest refinery complex and Qatar's LNG terminal have been completely shut down, with no timeline for restoration. More consequential still is Iran's threat to the Strait of Hormuz: nearly all of Iraq's, Kuwait's, Iran's own, and most of Saudi Arabia's and the UAE's oil exports pass through this chokepoint, as does virtually all of Qatar's LNG. Under normal conditions, roughly one-third of the world's seaborne crude oil and close to one-fifth of its liquefied natural gas transits through this strait.

Iran's large naval surface fleet may have been degraded, but its truly dangerous capability is not big warships — it's a low-cost lethality system built around mines and fast attack boats. Iran possesses enormous mine stockpiles that can be rapidly laid by swarms of small craft. Once the strait is mined and combined with drone and missile attacks on commercial shipping, the result is simple: no one dares to pass.

So the reality today is not a full blockade — it's that no one is willing to try. Shipping companies, facing skyrocketing insurance premiums and security risks, are choosing to reroute or suspend operations altogether. The result: vast quantities of oil and gas are stranded at source, with nowhere to go.

By the end of the war's first week, approximately 3,000 commercial vessels had been forced to linger in the region — around 10% of the global container fleet. Iraqi oil scheduled for export cannot move; storage capacity is hitting its limits, forcing output cuts. Kuwait and the UAE face similar pressures and have already begun discussing whether to shut down portions of their oil fields.

Saudi Arabia can reroute some of its oil through Red Sea pipelines, but most of its production still depends on Hormuz. A prolonged disruption would put enormous pressure on Riyadh as well.

If this situation continues for weeks, the impact won't simply be a modest rise in oil prices — it will be a global chain reaction. Crude prices have already surged sharply. European natural gas prices have spiked 70%.

The first to suffer will be countries heavily dependent on Middle Eastern energy: Malaysia, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and others are already feeling imported inflation. Rising oil prices push up transportation costs, which push up consumer prices, which drag down global economic growth.

And lurking behind the war is a hidden beneficiary: Russia. The higher oil and gas prices climb, the more Russia earns. That money will almost certainly flow back into the war in Ukraine, creating a vicious cycle.

This is ultimately a high-stakes economic counterattack — drag the global economy into the mud to force the US to blink. But it's also alienating everyone, including Gulf states that previously maintained decent relations with Tehran.

## Aviation in Paralysis

The aviation industry is another casualty. Dubai, Abu Dhabi, and Doha — some of the world's busiest aviation hubs — have all been hit to varying degrees. With missiles, drones, and fighter jets crisscrossing the airspace above the Middle East, safety cannot be guaranteed. Iraq, Qatar, Bahrain, Syria, Jordan, Israel, and even neighboring countries have instituted partial airspace restrictions.

By the end of the first week, more than 18,000 flights had been cancelled. The hardest hit are Emirates, Etihad, and Qatar Airways — carriers whose entire business model is built on the Middle Eastern hub-and-spoke system: fly global passengers into the Middle East, then distribute them to the world. That hub is now broken, replaced by an enormous no-fly zone.

Add to this the fact that Russian airspace has been closed since the Ukraine war, and global aviation routes have been squeezed into just two narrow corridors: one through Central Asia, one through India. These routes are longer, require clearances from more countries, and carry far higher coordination costs — all while fuel prices soar, delivering a double blow to carriers' cost structures.

To make matters worse, the backup Central Asian corridors may themselves become unsafe. Azerbaijan has its own tensions with Iran. If even these routes come under threat, the global aviation network will be compressed further still, stranding tens of thousands of passengers at airports around the world — a disruption that could persist for a very long time.

This is also hollowing out the foundations of the Gulf states' prosperity. Their rise over the past few decades was built on stability and security. That foundation is now their vulnerability.

The US government has called on American citizens in the Middle East to evacuate as soon as possible. The problem: there are no flights. Its suggested solution was remarkably tone-deaf: advise Americans in Israel to take buses to Egypt and fly home from there.

Roughly 12% of global air cargo and vast sections of high-value manufacturing supply chains depend on the Middle East aviation corridor. Air freight between Europe and China is now effectively paused. With Trump's tariff war already underway, the war in Iran is delivering a second simultaneous shock to global supply chains.

The longer-term implication: this war may strip Gulf carriers of their dominant position. A growing number of airlines are already considering shifting their route networks toward safer regions — India, East Asia — and may never return.

## Escalation Options Still Available

As bad as things already are, Iran still has many escalation options left. With more advanced missiles and a calculation that Gulf states' air defense systems are increasingly fatigued, Iran may be eyeing an extraordinarily sensitive target: desalination plants.

Kuwait gets roughly 90% of its drinking water from desalination. Saudi Arabia about 70%. The UAE around 42%. If these facilities are struck, this isn't a partial outage in one district — entire cities could become uninhabitable. A leaked US diplomatic cable from 2009 noted that if Saudi Arabia's desalination plants were destroyed, cities dependent on pipeline water supplies could face mass evacuation within a week. For Gulf states, this threat is nothing less than existential.

Within Gulf capitals, an intense debate is now underway: should they simply join the war directly, standing alongside the US and Israel to eliminate what remains of Iran's missile and drone capabilities?

Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar have all declared the right to self-defense, and their rhetoric is hardening. Options include opening their airspace to US forces or allowing US bases on their soil to conduct strikes.

But once more nations enter the fight, the airspace becomes far more chaotic, and the risk of miscalculation and friendly fire rises sharply. There have already been incidents — Kuwait's own air defense system accidentally hit a Kuwaiti oil field. More such accidents could bring far more serious consequences.

Europe, meanwhile, is being pulled in gradually. Iran fired missiles at a British military base in Cyprus; Britain subsequently authorized US use of its bases to conduct operations against Iran. The UK's base on Diego Garcia in the Indian Ocean has been reactivated — a critically important facility capable of supporting long-range missions including B-2 stealth bomber strikes. Greece has reinforced its military posture in the Mediterranean. If the conflict expands further, it could potentially trigger NATO's collective defense clause, theoretically obligating all member states to participate. The conflict would then risk escalating from a regional war into something far broader.

Beyond the main front, the war is metastasizing in other directions. Reports suggest the CIA is supporting armed groups in Iraq's Kurdish region, attempting to push them into Iran's Kurdish-populated borderlands. If Kurdish forces actually seize some territory, other minority groups may follow — the Azerbaijani minority in the north, the Baluch in the southeast.

Iran's population is roughly 60% Persian; the remaining 40% are various ethnic minorities. But they do not want the country dismembered. If they conclude that overthrowing the government means the country fragments like Yugoslavia — or falls into a Yugoslav-style civil war — many may change their minds, choosing to support a government they dislike rather than watch the state collapse. This is why, even as the US and Israel relentlessly strike Iran's leadership and military, there has been no mass nationwide uprising. The civilian population still places national unity above all else.

One more idea is circulating in Washington: seize Kharg Island. The island is small but hosts Iran's largest crude oil export terminal. Iran's own mainland ports are too shallow for supertankers; Kharg is not, making it the chokepoint for Iran's oil export economy — its primary source of foreign exchange revenue.

From Washington's perspective, if air strikes alone cannot bring Iran to its knees, economic strangulation — without a full-scale land invasion — might do it. A small amphibious force sounds far more manageable than hundreds of thousands of troops bogged down indefinitely.

But the operation is far harder than it sounds. US forces are already struggling to operate freely in the Strait of Hormuz. The assault would likely require airborne and amphibious operations launched from Kuwait or similar staging grounds. While US air power would dominate, Iranian missiles and drones would be well within range — the landing and occupation phase could incur significant casualties, with supply lines extremely difficult to maintain.

There are also second-order effects. Cutting off Iran's oil exports would push global oil prices even higher, intensifying pressure on shipping and potentially triggering strikes on alternatives to Hormuz — including desalination plants. If those are struck, Saudi Arabia and the UAE may feel compelled to join the war directly, making the situation even less controllable.

## Trump's Dilemma — and the World's

Even if Iran loses Kharg Island, it retains many escalation options. The seizure might bring Iran to the table, or it might simply make the war more brutal.

To truly resolve the Hormuz crisis requires either negotiation or decisively defeating Iran. This leaves Trump in an acutely uncomfortable position. If economic pressure forces him to stand down, he will have demonstrated to Iran — and to the world — that American military deterrence has limits. Israel wants a permanent solution to the Iran problem; if the US backs off, Israel may well continue unilaterally, causing the Gulf states to lose faith in Washington, since it was America that dragged them into this in the first place.

Stepping back would tell Iran that if you simply absorb the air strikes and strangle the shipping lanes, you can force America to blink. And it would tell them they can use that playbook again. It would accelerate the Gulf states' conclusion that the US is unreliable and they must find their own way — perhaps by seeking the protection of other great powers.

For Trump's domestic politics, the greater the global economic shock, the lower his approval ratings. American polling already shows limited public support for this war. If the economy enters a serious recession because of the conflict, Trump's political prospects would become very dim indeed.

In this environment, a growing number of countries may begin calling for a ceasefire and negotiations, driven purely by their own interests. This is one of those moments that could determine the direction of the next several decades. To break the deadlock, someone has to blink first.
